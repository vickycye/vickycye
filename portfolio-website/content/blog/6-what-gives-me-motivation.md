# "What gives you motivation?"

My best friend asked me a question: "What gives you motivation?"

That's a very broad question... Motivation is a word I frequently use, but one I rarely stop to think about. I recently learned that classical psychology offers two views on motivation: it's either *intrinsic* or *extrinsic*. The former is born from within, whereas the latter is pulled from the world around us. I think we mostly only talk about extrinsic motivation.

But after some thinking I have come to believe that for most people who are genuinely driven, there's a good mix of both. So, I asked myself 3 questions: What gets me started? What keeps me going? And what am I ultimately reaching for?

The first spark for me always comes from a person. There is a meaningful difference between envy and admiration, and I think it matters a lot which one you feel when you see someone extraordinary. Envy is wanting what someone has, and admiration is wanting to become who someone is. When I encounter someone whose life and work motivate me, whether they're decades older than me or somehow only 14, with a background that almost seems impossible, I don't feel diminished in any way. I get a rush of energy, and life seems brighter.

I think of researchers like Natasha Jaques, and she pursues work between ML and human wellbeing with a clear purpose. I think of Chinmay Nirkhe, who seems extremely passionate about his work, and who gives back and teaches his community in a inspiring way. I think of Ranjay Krishna, who has built himself into someone who not only excels at the frontier of his field, but does so surrounded by some of the finest mentorship in the world. When I see people like this, I always think: I could be like them someday. I hold them on a pedestal, not exactly to worship them (even if it might seem like it sometimes), but to orient myself in their direction. 

I think that is my extrinsic form of motivation. But it only provides a short burst of willpower.

What sustains me is a love of learning that I can only describe as indiscriminate.

I'm a loyal computer science student, and I feel at home in the CSE courses. But I still find myself equally drawn to philosophy, history, economics, medicine, or really anything that helps me understand how the world works and why people behave the way they do. I think that the most interesting problems don't live in a single discipline, and that a person who understands only their own field is, in someway, partially blind.

There's also a practical dimension to this. Breadth of understanding is definitely satisfying, but it also connects knowledge in a way I could never have known before. It allows me to sit across from someone whose world looks nothing like mine and to speak their language, and to understand what they care about and why. In a world that heavily rewards specialization, I think being a "jack of all trades" is undervalued. 

The hardest question yet is "what am I ultimately reaching for?"

I don't really know. I don't have a crytallized version of where I'm going, because the world evolves so fast and what I dream about today might be completely different a month from now. But I do have something I believe is more durable than a goal, which is a direction, and a set of values. I want to contribute to the world in a way what is meaningful, realistically helpful (not helpful in a performative sense, but in the way that really understanding things can cause good change). And I want someday, to be someone that a younger person looks at the way I look at the people who inspire me.

Maybe motivation isn't about destination. The figures that I admired for so long didn't arrive at their destination overnight. They accumulated it through sustained curiousity, through the right connections, and through showing up and consistenty putting in effort again and again for the work they believed in. Thinking about that gets me out of bed every morning and brings me to the lecture halls. I don't know exactly where I'm headed to. But, I do know what gets me started, I know what keeps me going, and I know the vague shape of the life I'm reaching toward. 

For now, maybe that's enough.
