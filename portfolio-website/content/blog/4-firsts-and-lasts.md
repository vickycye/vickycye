# Firsts and Lasts

I didn't think about it until someone pointed it out.

It was the week before graduation, and a friend said something offhand. It was something like, "haha this is the last time I'll ever see you." And suddenly I couldn't stop noticing. 

We spend so much of our lives chasing firsts. First days, first wins, first times doing something that scares us. Firsts feel like beginnings, and beginnings feel like promise. Nobody tells you to pay attention to the lasts.

#### A Life Full of Firsts

I had spent four years collecting firsts without realizing it. The first orchestra concert, where, for reasons I can no longer fully explain, I dressed up as Ghostface. The first day I finally met my covid online freshman friends in person. 

Those firsts announced themselves boldly. They had a quality of *newness* that made them easy to hold onto. But I didn't think to cherish the quieter ones: the last time I walked out of a classroom I'd sat in for a year, the last time I passed someone in the hallway who would eventually just... stop being part of my daily life. Those didn't feel like anything at the time. They just felt like Tuesdays.

That's the thing about lasts, most of them are disguised as insignificant, ordinary moments.

#### The Weight of Lasts
Once I started noticing, I couldn't stop.

It was the last time I'd drive through that same pothole at the entrance of the parking lot. The last time I'd step into the orchestra room at 7:20am, the room still smelling like rosin from the day before. The last time I'd sit in the history classroom playing TFT with my friends while we should've been doing something else. That last time we'd somehow set off a minor catastrophe trying to make mac and cheese in the prep room of the biotech classroom, the kind of memory that sounds insane out of context and yet makes perfect sense to everyone who was there.

Lasts are quite different. Lasts have weight.

#### ...

The same day my friend made her offhand joke happened to be the night of our last orchestra concert.

We played the Halo Theme and the Legend of Zelda. I remember standing on stage, receiving my graduation cord, and feeling something different. Afterward, we said our goodbyes as "old seniors", and then I drove home.

Somewhere along that drive, tears started rolling down my face.

I wasn't expecting it. But I think some part of me had finally understood what I'd been circling all week. It wasn't specifically the concert, or the cord, or even the people. It was the realization that I would never again feel exactly *that*. *That* specific sense of community, built slowly over three years with those exact people in that exact room. *That* version of belonging was closing, and nothing on the other side would be quite its shape. 

There's something fitting about the traditions of senior sunrise and sunset. You gather at the beginning of the first day to watch something begin, and again at the end to watch it all go.

I didn't fully understand the tradition until I was participating in it. Sunrises and sunsets don't really matter. I think it was about choosing, for once, to mark the moment. To show up and confidently say: "I know this is ending, and I'm here for it anyway."

#### College!

The last post I wrote was also about college, but it's a great time for reflection. College arrived the way new chapters do. It was extremely abrupt, and with no regard for whether you felt ready.

Boom! New building. New faces. Ugly tables in lecture halls. It was the same feeling of not quite knowing where to place yourself. But I noticed, pretty quickly, that I was already collecting firsts again. A new set of people I'd only just met. Classrooms I was walking into for the first time. 

And I understood that this is how it works. Every chapter starts with firsts. If you're lucky, you stay long enough to feel the lasts. The difference is now I know they're coming. So I can try to pay attention earlier.

Every year, you become someone slightly different. The person I was as a freshman in high school is not quite who I am now. And next year, I'll look back at who I am today with the same mixture of tenderness and distance.

I think the temptation is to be embarrassed by the earlier versions. To cringe at the choices, the phases, and the earnestness. But those versions were doing the best they could with what they had. They were collecting experiences that would eventually, quietly, become the story you tell.

So don't regret who you were. Don't rush past who you are. And don't be so fixed on who you're becoming that you forget to notice the random Tuesday nights, the parking lot potholes, and the 7:20am orchestra rooms. The ordinary moments make the whole thing.

The firsts will keep coming. So will the lasts. The only difference between the two is whether you're paying attention in the moment.
